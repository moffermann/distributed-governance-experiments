# Satellite Paper — Outline and Claims-to-Evidence Map (Phase 0)

Working title: **Stress-Testing a Distributed Public-Resource Governance Architecture: Adversarial and Behavioral Agent-Based Evidence**

Spine decision: **findings-first with a load-bearing methods section**. The findings carry the governance interest; the methods (pre-registration, traceability contract, LLM calibration, cross-engine and cross-framework docking) carry the credibility. This fits both venue families below; a JASSS submission reweights Section 2 upward without restructuring.

1. **JASSS** (Journal of Artificial Societies and Social Simulation) — natural home: ABM of institutions, values docking and pre-registration; open access, no APC.
2. **Policy & Internet / Digital Government: Research and Practice** — governance-first framing, methods as support.

Backup: PLOS ONE (scope-agnostic, reproducibility-friendly).

## Section outline

1. **Introduction** — the master architecture (cite the working paper DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21228492), what simulation can and cannot establish (simulation evidence, not institutional proof), the six questions this paper answers, contributions.
2. **Methods** — the framework contract: deterministic engines, paired seeds, common worlds; the Core v0 traceability matrix as an anchoring discipline; pre-registration with honest prediction accounting (refuted-informative as first-class outcome); the LLM-elicited-priors calibration method and its declared epistemic status; version-stamped outputs and regression anchors; engines A (adversarial static), B (behavioral adoption), planning-vector, E (longitudinal v0.6–v0.8).
3. **Finding 1: the architecture ranking is behavioral-population-invariant** (Experiments A+C) — 2.0–2.7×; no-default participation is non-functioning, not weaker.
4. **Finding 2: planning-vector quality dominates, and distributed construction wins where its preconditions hold** (planning-vector program; ladder 0.815–0.939 vs 0.315–0.650 vs 0.187).
5. **Finding 3: the attentive share is structural (~2.5–5%)** (Experiment B + LLM panels; convergence at N=1000; mean-sufficiency; the master's E8 bridge as external validation).
6. **Finding 4: the deterrence stack is individually redundant and jointly indispensable** (ablation; K10; the docs/111 resolution as knowledge transfer back to the architecture) — plus attacks: the choke-point price vs distributed robustness.
7. **Finding 5: the semi-open transition is a dial, not a leap** (fiscal-parallel blend; break-even 8–10%).
8. **Finding 6: the pipeline view — release policy, verification ceiling, AI triage, adaptive adversaries** (Experiment E program): pull rule; Little's-Law W\*; AI as capacity insurance with stack-slack absorbing verifier error; windows fix the tail; adaptivity neither erodes nor inverts; verification is downstream of deterrence.
9. **Cross-validation** — the Mesa dock (external, this paper's docking report); internal cross-engine docks (degraded-stack longitudinal 0.114 ≈ static K10 0.110; E8 behavioral bridge 2.26 ≈ imposed 2.22); statistical annex.
10. **Limitations and declared boundaries** — synthetic priors pending the identical human instrument; honest-elicitation boundary; no per-project veto stage; audit-anchored parameter provenance; single-scope worlds.
11. **Conclusions** — the three-line institutional summary (size the capacity, meter the release, protect the margin) and the two-way street: findings that already traveled to the master corpus through its adversarial pipeline (docs/110's quantification, docs/111), and the queue that awaits (release rule, verification package, verifier displacement).
